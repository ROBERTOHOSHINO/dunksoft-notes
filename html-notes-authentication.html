01
The Problem She Solved

In the late 1990s, my mother-in-law was the office director of a law firm specializing in debt restructuring. Her job: stand between hundreds of people crushed by multiple loans and the financial institutions that held their debt.

Every month, she handwrote approximately 400 bank transfer slips — payment by payment, creditor by creditor, five days and ten days at a time. Then she carried them to the bank.

The System Before
400 handwritten transfer slips per cycle → physical trips to the bank → error-prone, time-consuming, not scalable.

One person. One pen. Hundreds of lives depending on each stroke.

At that time, I was programming the communications layer for Sumitomo Bank ATM terminals. I understood exactly how the data sat inside those systems. And I had been writing extensively in Microsoft ACCESS.

The solution was straightforward: build a database of each debtor's repayment plan, generate the per-creditor transfer data automatically, and export it in the format the bank could read directly.

The key insight came from Fuji Bank's Kudan branch (now part of Mizuho): if we embedded a debtor reference number in the memo field of each transfer record, creditors could reconcile payments automatically on their end.

"One field in a memo column changed everything."

With that single specification addition, the entire process digitized. The law firm grew rapidly. The branch manager was promoted — significantly, I imagine. Transfer fees dropped as volume moved from tellers to data transmission. Processing time collapsed.

This is what optimization means when it reaches the right place. Not just efficiency — liberation. She could focus on the people, not the paper.

02
The Problem That Stopped Her

She was in her fifties when she learned to use a computer. Not reluctantly — enthusiastically. She built her own Excel spreadsheets for personal asset management. She moved from a feature phone to a smartphone ahead of most people her age.

Then authentication stopped her.

Not a sudden failure. A slow retreat. Passwords grew longer and more complex. IDs multiplied across services. The gap between what the systems demanded and what human memory could hold kept widening. Gradually, she could no longer get in.

"She didn't forget how to think. She forgot which string of characters she had invented last time."

She is not alone. Kubo-san, who appears on our radio show as our elder commentator, faces the same wall. Millions of seniors who have decades of knowledge and wisdom to share are being quietly locked out of the systems that could help them share it.

03
The Technical Root of the Problem

I learned about Integrity early in my programming career — the principle that data must be consistent, valid, and trustworthy throughout a system. The moment I internalized it, I started noticing a quiet anomaly in Japanese digital infrastructure.

Technical Note — Integrity Failures in Japanese Authentication
Full-width alphanumerics inside Japanese text environments (Ａ Ｂ Ｃ vs A B C) are treated as different characters — but look identical to users. A password entered in one mode fails in another. The user is blamed.

ASCII codes from 30h onward include characters that appear numeric but are classified as text. Systems built without accounting for this create silent authentication failures that users cannot diagnose.

Authentication — the most critical gateway in any system — is being built on top of these unresolved inconsistencies.

The current authentication paradigm asks people to remember something arbitrary: a string of characters invented under pressure, forgotten under time. This is not a memory problem. It is a design problem — and it systematically excludes the people who most need access: the elderly, children, non-native language users.

04
The Mission Forward

What if authentication was built on what you cannot forget — because it is woven into who you are?

The color of the indigo at the Kanda festival. The yellow of the roses at Yanagimori Shrine. The deep green of a Murano glass ring from Venice, bought after 3.11. These are not passwords. They are life itself.

The senior population holds irreplaceable knowledge. AI is now powerful enough to help them share it — but only if they can get through the door. Japan's government is deploying Claude and the next versions of GPT at scale. The question is not whether AI will be used. The question is whether the people who most need it can reach it.
